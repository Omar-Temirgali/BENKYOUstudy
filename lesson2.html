Equivalent of a verb to be is the copulative です verb.

Japanese grammar is quite simple and straight forward but very different from English grammar so most English speakers find it rather confusing. For instance, in Japanese the verb always comes at the end. The best thing you can do when learning Japanese is to learn it from the bottom up and not compare it to English grammar.

The Japanese language uses what we will refer to as particles to mark the various parts of the sentence.

Keep in mind that the Japanese language has different levels of politeness that change based on who you are talking to. I will refer to these as "ultra-formal", "formal" (or "polite form"), "normal" (or "plain form"), "informal", and "rude" (we won't be covering too much of the rude form, however).

Let's start from pronouns:


わwaたtaしshi I
わwaたtaしshi た ta ちchi we
あaなnaたta you, You
あaなnaたta た ta ちchi you (plural)
あaのno人hito （ あa のno方kata ） he, she (this human)
かkaれre he
かkaのnoじょjo she
かkaれreらra they

Let's pay attention to a couple of points:

1. You might have noticed a strange combination in the form he/she あ aのno人 hito ( あaのno方kata ). Since there is no such thing as gender in Japanese, a common word is used to denote the words he and she - あ a の no 人 hito, literally that person. This formula is used in both neutral, colloquial and formal speech.


2. You probably noticed that in order to get the plural form, it is enough to use the suffix たち or ら, which we attach to the word itself.


In order to say in Japanese "I am John", "I am a designer", etc., the following construction is used:


NOUN1 はwa NOUN2 でde すsu。
I はwa designer am
わwaたtaしshi はwa デdeザza イiナna ーa でde すsu。

In the middle of a sentence - immediately after the first noun, the particle は is added. The particle "wa" marks the topic of the sentence.

Examples:

わたしはジョンです。 (watashi wa jon desu) - I'm John.

父はエンジニアです。 (chichi wa enjinia desu) - My father is an engineer.

今日は金曜日です。 (kyou wa kinyoubi desu) - Today is Friday.

あの人は医者です。 (anohito wa ishya desu) - He/she is a doctor.


The same construction can be used to say about the location of something or someone in a certain place.

妹は今日本です。 (imouto wa ima nihon desu) - My little sister is in Japan now.

わたしたちはもう外です。 (watashitachi wa mou soto desu) - We are already outside.

夫は仕事です。 (otto wa shigoto desu) - Husband is at work.


Instead of NOUN2 we can use adjectives.

彼はかっこいいです。 (kare wa kakkoii desu) - He is handsome.

エレナは面白いです。 (Elena wa omoshiroi desu) - Elena is funny.


Negations

It is very simple - at the end of the sentence, instead of です, there is a bundle ではありません (dewaarimasen). Nothing else changes.


NOUN1 はwa NOUN2 でde はwa あa りri まma せse んn。
I はwa designer not
わwaたtaしshi はwa デdeザza イiナna ーa でde はwa あa りri まma せse んn。

In conversations, じゃありません (jaarimasen) expression is often used instead of the expression では ありません.

Examples:

今日は金曜日じゃありません。 (kyou wa kinyoubi jaarimasen) - Today is not Friday.

これはわたし の かばんじゃありません。 (kore wa watashi no kaban jaarimasen) - This is not my bag.

これは家じゃありません。アパートです。 (kore wa ie jaarimasen. apaato desu) - This is not a house. It is an apartment.

あれは病院じゃありません。学校です。 (are wa byouin jaarimasen. gakkou desu) - It is not a hospital over there. There is a school.


Questions

To ask a question, after the word です we put the question mark か. And that's it. The question is ready. There is nothing more to change. The question mark is not used when using the か particle in Japanese.


NOUN1 はwa NOUN2 で de すsu　 かka。

あなたはがくせいです か。 (anata wa gakusei desu ka) - Are you a student?

明日は金曜日です か。 (ashita wa kinyoubi desu ka) - Tomorrow is Friday?

山田さんは今日、 忙しいです か。 (Yamada-san wa kyou, isogashii desu ka) - Yamada-san, are you busy today?


The answer to this question should begin with はい (yes) or いいえ, 違います (no).

― あaはnaたta　は wa　学gaku生sei　で de すsu　かka。 – Are you a student?

― はha い i 、 学gaku生sei　 で de すsu。 – Yes, I am.

or

― いi い i えe 、 学gaku生 sei　じゃja あa り ri まma せse んn。 – No, I am not a student.


More examples:

― こkoこko　はwa　銀gin行kou　で de すsu　かka。 – Is this ("here") a bank?

― 違chiga いi まmaすsu 、 市shi役yaku所sho 　 でdeすsu。 – No, this is the City Hall.


― 今日kyou 　はwa　火ka曜you日bi 　でde すsu　か ka。 – Today is Tuesday?

― い i いi えe 、 火 ka曜you日bi　じゃja あa りri まma せse んn。 月getsu 曜you日bi　でde す su。 – No, it isn't. It's Monday.


Questions with question words

In Japanese, the order of words in question sentences with question words is the same as in a regular sentence. The word to which the question is asked is replaced by the corresponding question word. Question words most often come after は . At the end of a sentence, after です or another verb, the question particle か is placed.

Some question words:


Who – だda れre（more polite version – どdo なna た ta ）

What – なna ん n ／ なna にni

Where – どdo こko

Which of – どdoれre

When – いi つtsu

How much (about cost) – いi くku ら ra


Examples:

中chuu華ka料ryou理ri のno　レreスsuトto ラraンn　はwa　どdoこko　 でde すsu　かka。 – Where is the restaurant of Chinese cuisine?

こkoれre　はwa　何nan　でde すsu 　かka。 – What is this?


In the answers we substitute the answer instead of the question word. The order of words does not change.

― トtoイiレre　はwa 　 どdo こko　でde すsu　かka。 – Where is the restroom?

― トtoイiレre　はwa 　向muこkoうu　でde す su。 – The restroom is over there.


In Japanese, possessive pronouns are formed very simply - we need to add a particle の to the usual personal pronouns.

わwaたtaしshi　の no - my/mine

かkaのnoじょjo　の no - her/hers


Examples:
